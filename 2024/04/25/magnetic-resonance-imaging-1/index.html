1. The attempt of a gentle introduction

1.1. The three principles

1.1.1. Principle 1: Proton magnetization

  • If protons reside in a magnetic field $\boldsymbol{B}_0$, a proton magnetization $\boldsymbol{M}||\boldsymbol{B}_0$.

  • MRI is based on sampling the proton magnetization $\boldsymbol{M}$.

  • $M \propto B_0$

1.1.2. Principle 2: Magnetization generates a magnetic dipole field

  • $\boldsymbol{M}$ generates a magnetic dipole field $\boldsymbol{B_1^-(r)}$.

  • MRI is based on measuring $\boldsymbol{B_1^-(r)}$ via magnetic induction.

1.1.3. Principle 3: Magnetic resonance

  • If a proton magnetization $\boldsymbol{M}$ is present in a sample placed in $\boldsymbol{B}_0$ and if an oscillatory magnetic field $\boldsymbol{B_1^+}$ is irradiated and if the frequency of $\boldsymbol{B_1^+}$ is $v_0 = \frac{1}{2\pi}\gamma B_0$ , then $\boldsymbol{M}$ will irradiate back an oscillatory magnetic field $\boldsymbol{B_1^-}$ of ferquency $v_0$ .

  • The $\boldsymbol{B_1^-}$ field gradually becomes weaker. It does so exponentially with a decay time $T_2$​​ , which is tissue specific.

  • The difference between $\boldsymbol{M_0}$ and $\boldsymbol{M_{||}}(t_0)$ diminishs exponentially like $(M_0-M_{||}(t_0))·exp(-(t-t_0)/T_1)$. $T_1$ is tissue-specific.
